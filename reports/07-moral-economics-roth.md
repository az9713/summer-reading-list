## 2. Core thesis

Roth's organizing concept is **repugnance** - his long-running research idea that some voluntary transactions between consenting parties are blocked not because they cause measurable harm to others, but because third parties find them morally or religiously objectionable. These are "repugnant transactions" or "contested markets."

The book's central, repeated argument (per publisher copy and the Stanford write-ups):

- **Contested markets cannot really be abolished - only relocated.** Banning a transaction tends to drive it underground, push it across borders (e.g., transplant tourism, surrogacy tourism), or leave it operating informally and dangerously, rather than making it disappear.
- **Therefore markets are better designed honestly than denied.** "Prudent market design" can find a balance between preserving people's right to pursue their own interests and protecting the most vulnerable from harm. Markets "don't have to allow everything or ban everything."
- **Move the debate from denunciation to evidence.** Roth advocates an empiricist, benefits-and-costs, trade-off approach - evaluating alternative legal regimes with experiments and cross-country comparisons rather than purely moral absolutes. As one summary put it: keep asking, *with evidence rather than absolutes*, how the tools (markets) should be built.

This is the through-line to his prizewinning work: a market is "a tool to help decide who gets what," and the job of *moral* economics is to design those tools well even - especially - where society is morally divided.

## 3. Themes and examples it covers

Reported subject matter spans (from publisher copy, Stanford/SIEPR, and the PW review):

- Sex work / prostitution
- Surrogacy and adoption
- Organ sales and **kidney exchange / paired donation and donor chains** (Roth's signature applied work)
- Blood and blood-plasma markets
- Alcohol, **marijuana / drugs**
- **Vaccine challenge trials**
- **Medical aid in dying / assisted suicide**
- Abortion
- (Roth's broader corpus also touches same-sex marriage and other shifting-repugnance cases as norms change over time)

A recurring analytical motif is that **repugnance is not fixed** - markets that were once banned become accepted (and vice versa), so the interesting questions are about *how non-market norms form, hold, and sometimes collapse*, and how to design the legal/market regime around them.

## 4. Relation to Roth's Nobel-winning market-design work

Roth won the 2012 Nobel for the theory and practice of **market design / matching markets** (deferred-acceptance matching, with Shapley's theory). His applied landmarks include the National Resident Matching Program redesign, school-choice systems (Boston, NYC), and especially **kidney exchange** - algorithms enabling paired donations and donor chains, reported in the *New England Journal of Medicine* (2009), credited with 10,000+ kidney-exchange transplants worldwide.

*Moral Economics* is the explicitly normative extension of that program: kidney exchange exists precisely **because** buying and selling kidneys is repugnant/illegal in most places, so market designers had to engineer a non-monetary matching market instead. The book generalizes that lesson - when a straightforward market is morally blocked, good design finds a workable, ethically defensible alternative rather than pretending the demand goes away.

- **Publishers Weekly (pre-pub review, ~March 2026):** Positive - calls it a "stimulating study of morally contested products and services," and concludes: *"Bringing balanced, evidence-based analyses to emotionally fraught debates, Roth reveals the power of markets to inspire solutions. This is trailblazing."*
- **David Thesmar (economist, MIT Sloan)** publicly reviewed it, calling it "truly entertaining and thought-provoking… in the line of his reflections on repugnant markets. Highly recommend!" while noting "a few disagreements."

No major long-form newspaper book reviews were confirmed at the time of this report (book is very new).

## 6. Who it's for

- General readers interested in the ethics of markets and "things money shouldn't buy" debates (a natural companion/counterpoint to Michael Sandel's *What Money Can't Buy*).
- Economists, market/mechanism designers, and law-and-economics readers.
- Policy people working on transplantation, drug policy, sex-work law, surrogacy, end-of-life care.
- The a16z-crypto-adjacent audience (it was recommended by **Scott Duke Kominers**, Roth's former doctoral student) thinking about **when markets are allowed to exist, how to design them under contested norms, and how social norms emerge and collapse** - directly relevant to token markets, prediction markets, and other "is this even allowed to be a market?" questions.

---

## 7. Why a markets / mechanism-design reader might value it

- It treats **repugnance as a design constraint**, not just a moral footnote - a concrete framework for "this transaction is blocked; what mechanism still clears the need?"
- The kidney-exchange story is the canonical worked example of routing around a forbidden price mechanism with a matching mechanism - a transferable pattern.
- Its "you can't ban a market, only relocate it" thesis is a sharp heuristic for anyone designing or regulating markets (including crypto/permissionless ones): prohibition reshapes, rather than eliminates, the market.
- It pairs an empirical, comparative-policy method with mechanism design - bridging "what works" with "what's permitted."

# The Art and Craft of Problem Solving - Paul Zeitz (3rd ed.)

**Bottom line (verdict)**
- *What it is:* A graduate-of-the-Olympiad's training manual for **how to solve hard math problems** - organized around transferable *strategies, tactics, and tools* rather than memorized facts, spanning algebra, combinatorics, number theory, geometry, and calculus.
- *Who should read it:* Mathematically curious people who want to **sharpen reasoning and investigation skills** - competition students (AIME/Putnam/IMO), undergraduates, and self-learners willing to struggle productively. Not a reference text and not for someone wanting worked-out answers handed over.
- *Time investment:* A ~366–384 page book, but it is **worked, not read** - realistically months of active practice if you do the problems. Skimming the four foundational chapters alone (~a few evenings) already conveys the method.

---

## What the book is about and its pedagogical approach

Zeitz's central premise is a distinction between an **exercise** (you already know a procedure; you just execute it) and a **problem** (you do *not* have an immediate path to the answer and must investigate). The book's stated goal is to develop genuine problem-solving skill by encouraging students to **"do" math rather than just study it** - a problem-solving approach as opposed to the traditional exercises-and-drills approach. ([Wiley product page](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Art+and+Craft+of+Problem+Solving,+3rd+Edition-p-9781119239901), as summarized in search; [MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))

What reviewers single out as distinctive is that the book **teaches methodology, not just a problem dump**: sample problems are "thoroughly explored and investigated," showing *how* a solver arrives at a solution - the messy search process - rather than presenting a polished final answer. ([MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))

Zeitz also foregrounds the **psychological dimension** of problem solving - confidence, concentration, persistence, and creativity ("courage") - arguing that success depends as much on mindset and a willingness to not attack a problem head-on as on technique. He uses motivational devices (e.g., the "Mouse Story") to make the point. ([book summaries/Goodreads-adjacent sources](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/593458.The_Art_and_Craft_of_Problem_Solving))

## Main themes and structure: strategies vs. tactics vs. tools

The book's signature organizing idea is a three-level hierarchy of problem-solving methods (an explicit analogy to military thinking):

- **Strategies** - broad, non-technical orientations for opening up a problem (e.g., get your hands dirty, penultimate step, wishful thinking, make it easier). You think strategically *first*, on a "less focused level," before trying to solve. ([summary sources](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/593458.The_Art_and_Craft_of_Problem_Solving))
- **Tactics** - more focused, broadly applicable techniques such as symmetry, the extreme principle, invariants, and the pigeonhole principle.
- **Tools** - narrow, technical methods specific to a subject area.

Per the MAA review and publisher summaries, the **first four chapters** build the reader's general "toolkit" of strategies and tactics (including a chapter on important *crossover* tactics that recur across subjects). The book then devotes one chapter each to the **four main problem categories - algebra, combinatorics, number theory, and geometry - and concludes with a chapter on calculus.** ([MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))

Approximate chapter arc (foundations → subject areas):
1. What this book is about / how to read it
2. Strategies for investigating problems
3. Tactics for solving problems
4. Crossover tactics
5. Algebra
6. Combinatorics
7. Number theory
8. Geometry
9. Calculus

*(The foundations-then-five-subject-areas structure is confirmed by the MAA review; the exact chapter titles above are the well-known structure of the book and should be treated as approximate - see Open questions.)*

**Problem types.** Problems come in three flavors, deliberately mixed: **recreational/classic** (creative puzzles), **contest** (formal problems sourced from AIME, IMO, Putnam, etc., which can be quite hard), and **exploration** (open-ended problems that may have no clean answer - described by reviewers as the most challenging but most rewarding). ([MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))

## Difficulty, prerequisites, and audience

- **Prerequisites:** High-school algebra and geometry plus mathematical maturity/motivation. The calculus chapter assumes basic calculus, but most of the book does not. Reviewers note prerequisites are not heavy on paper - the difficulty comes from the *problems*, not from required background. ([MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))
- **Difficulty:** Ranges from approachable to very hard (IMO/Putnam-level). It is designed to stretch the reader.
- **Audience:** Aimed at **competition-math students, undergraduates, and self-learners**, as well as teachers/coaches. Zeitz's coaching background (USAMO, IMO teams) directly shapes the contest orientation. ([Wiley summary](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Art+and+Craft+of+Problem+Solving,+3rd+Edition-p-9781119239901); [MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))
- **Caveat for self-study:** A repeatedly noted limitation is that the **end-of-chapter problems largely lack full solutions** in the book itself - a deliberate pedagogical choice that frustrates some self-learners. ([MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))

## Author

**Paul Zeitz** (b. 1958) is a professor of mathematics at the **University of San Francisco**, specializing in math enrichment. He **won the USA Mathematical Olympiad (USAMO) in 1974** and was on the **first U.S. team at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO)** that year. He studied history at Harvard (BA, 1981) and earned a **PhD in mathematics from UC Berkeley (1992)** in ergodic theory. He has composed/edited problems for national contests including the USAMO and helped train U.S. IMO teams - most notably the **1994 "Dream Team," the first to achieve a perfect score**. He received the **MAA's Deborah Tepper Haimo award** for distinguished college teaching (2003) and co-founded **Proof School** (2015). He has also recorded a related course ("The Art and Craft of Mathematical Problem Solving") for The Great Courses. ([search-sourced Wikipedia biography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Zeitz); [The Great Courses author page](https://plus.thegreatcourses.com/paul-zeitz))

## Reception and reputation

- **Goodreads:** Reported average **~4.59 / 5 across ~267 ratings** - very strong. Reviewers call it something that "should be on the shelf of every problem solver" and "the single best resource for any high schooler looking to get into olympiad math." ([Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/593458.The_Art_and_Craft_of_Problem_Solving))
- **MAA Review (Jack Chen, 2016):** Calls it "an excellent gateway to the culture of problem solving," "challenging and rewarding," that "shines a new light on mathematics." Praises the emphasis on *methodology*; the main criticism is the lack of solutions to the problem sets. ([MAA Review](https://old.maa.org/press/maa-reviews/the-art-and-craft-of-problem-solving))
- **Community standing:** Widely regarded in the olympiad/competition-math community as a standard "bridge" text into serious problem solving. A common, nuanced take (e.g., on Quora) is that it is "**excellent and often essential, but by itself not sufficient**" to medal at the IMO - it must be paired with focused practice, advanced theory, and mentorship. ([Quora discussion](https://www.quora.com/Is-studying-Paul-Zeitz-The-Art-and-Craft-of-Problem-Solving-sufficient-to-get-you-a-medal-in-IMO))

## Why a technical reader sharpening reasoning might value it

This is the throughline that matches the a16z/Kominers recommendation ("fine-tune mathematical thinking and problem-solving"):

- It teaches a **domain-general method for attacking unfamiliar problems** - orient before attacking, run experiments ("get your hands dirty"), exploit symmetry/invariants/extremes, work backward from the goal - which transfers directly to research, algorithm design, cryptography, and any open-ended technical reasoning.
- It explicitly trains the **investigation process and the right psychological posture** (tolerance for not-knowing, persistence), which is exactly the skill that distinguishes routine execution from genuine problem solving.
- The strategy/tactic/tool taxonomy gives a **reusable mental checklist** rather than subject trivia, so the value survives even if you never touch a contest.

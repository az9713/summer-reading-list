# How Music Got Free - Stephen Witt (2015)

## Bottom line
- **What it is:** A narrative-nonfiction history of how digital technology (the MP3, then file-sharing, then streaming) dismantled the recorded-music business, told through three interlocking true stories - the German engineers who invented the MP3, the CD-plant worker who became piracy's "patient zero," and the major-label CEO who fought the collapse.
- **Who should read it:** Anyone interested in technology obliterating incumbent business models - investors, founders, product people, and music/media fans. It's a strong case study for a tech reader thinking about disruption.
- **Time investment:** \~290–300 pages / roughly an 8–9 hour read (or \~9.5 hour audiobook). Reads fast - reviewers repeatedly compare it to Michael Lewis.

---

## What the book is about

`How Music Got Free` braids three storylines that together explain how recorded music went from a high-margin physical product to something effectively free, then re-monetized through streaming:

1. **The inventors (Fraunhofer, Germany).** A research team at the Fraunhofer Society - led by **Karlheinz Brandenburg**, with colleagues including **Bernhard Grill** and **Harald Popp** - spent years developing the audio-compression algorithm that became the MP3, shrinking CD-quality audio to roughly one-twelfth its size. The book frames the MP3 as a near-commercial failure that lost the official standards battle (to formats backed by bigger players) but won the world anyway once it leaked into the piracy underground. *(Names verified via Wikipedia and the New Yorker reporting.)*

2. **The leaker (North Carolina).** **Dell Glover** - full name **Bennie Lydell Glover**, of Shelby, North Carolina - worked at the PolyGram/Universal CD-manufacturing plant in Kings Mountain, NC. From roughly 2001 until his 2007 arrest he smuggled pre-release CDs out of the factory and fed them to an online "warez" crew called **RNS (Rabid Neurosis)**, becoming the single largest source of leaked pre-release albums in the world - reportedly close to 2,000 albums over about eight years (Eminem, Kanye West, Jay-Z, Mariah Carey, and many more). He pleaded guilty to felony conspiracy to commit copyright infringement and served a short prison term. He is the book's "patient zero of piracy." *(Both names verified; "Dell Glover" is the everyday name, "Bennie Lydell Glover" the legal name.)*

3. **The executive (the industry).** **Doug Morris**, the longtime major-label chief (CEO of Universal Music Group during the period covered, later Sony Music), stands in for the industry's response - denial, lawsuits, and eventually the pivot to new models. The book uses him to track the business side: the collapse of CD revenue and the slow, painful migration toward iTunes, then ad-supported and subscription streaming. *(Name verified.)*

The connective tissue the a16z list highlights - MP3 → mass piracy (Napster/Kazaa/LimeWire) → iTunes → YouTube/VEVO → streaming - is exactly the arc Witt traces, with the human stories making the technology and economics concrete.

## Themes a disruption-minded reader will care about
- **Technology destroying a business model from the outside.** The people who broke the music industry weren't competitors with a better label - they were an engineer optimizing a codec and a factory temp with access and a fast internet connection. Disruption came from outside the industry's field of vision.
- **Standards, formats, and adoption.** The MP3 "lost" the formal standards process yet became ubiquitous - a vivid lesson in how distribution and real-world adoption beat official endorsement.
- **Incumbent response under disruption.** Litigation and DRM vs. eventually accepting new economics (downloads, then streaming) - a recurring pattern in tech-disrupted industries.
- **The economics of "free."** What happens to an entire industry when the marginal cost of a perfect copy goes to zero, and how value re-aggregates (platforms, subscriptions) afterward.

## Structure and reporting basis
- **Form:** Braided narrative nonfiction - three parallel character-driven threads, intercut and advancing chronologically from the early-1990s invention of the MP3 through the rise of streaming.
- **Reporting:** Built on original investigative reporting, including direct access to and interviews with Dell Glover and figures from the piracy scene, plus the technologists and industry executives. The book grew out of Witt's New Yorker feature **"The Man Who Broke the Music Business"** (April 2015), which centered on Glover. Reviewers singled out the freshness of the on-the-ground reporting (the leaker's story had not been told in this depth before).

## Author background - Stephen Witt
- **Stephen Richard Witt**, American journalist/nonfiction author, born 1979 (New Hampshire; raised in Minnesota).
- Degrees from the **University of Chicago** (2001) and the **Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism** (2011). Before journalism he worked \~six years in finance (stock trading / hedge funds) and did a stint in economic development in East Africa.
- Contributor to **The New Yorker** and other outlets. `How Music Got Free` was his debut book.
- Later wrote **`The Thinking Machine`** (2025), a history of Nvidia and Jensen Huang, which won the 2025 Financial Times Business Book of the Year Award.

## Reception and recognition
- **Critical reception:** Strongly positive. The New York Times' Dwight Garner praised its "clear writing and brisk reportorial acumen of a Michael Lewis book" and the paper called it "the richest explanation to date about how the arrival of the MP3 upended almost everything about how music is distributed, consumed and stored." The Washington Post called it "whip-smart, superbly reported and indispensable"; Time called it "lucid, mordantly funny."
- **Awards / lists (shortlists, not wins):**
  - Shortlisted, **2015 Financial Times and McKinsey Business Book of the Year Award** (also on that year's longlist).
  - Shortlisted, **2016 J. Anthony Lukas Book Prize**.
  - Finalist/shortlist, **2015 Los Angeles Times Book Prize** (Current Interest category).
  - Widely named to "best books of 2015" lists.
- **Adaptation:** A two-part documentary directed by Alex Stapleton (2024) draws on the book, with interviews including Eminem, Timbaland, and Jimmy Iovine.

## Open questions / uncertainties
- **Page count:** Wikipedia lists 296 pages; Amazon/Penguin list 304. The difference is edition/front-matter counting, not a substantive discrepancy.
- **Doug Morris's exact title during the events:** He led Universal Music Group during the period the book covers and later became CEO of Sony Music; sources here describe him as the major-label/Universal chief. Precise title-by-year was not independently nailed down beyond "CEO of Universal Music Group."

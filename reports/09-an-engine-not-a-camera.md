# An Engine, Not a Camera: How Financial Models Shape Markets - Donald MacKenzie (2006)

## Bottom line
- **What it is:** A rigorously documented work of economic sociology / science-and-technology studies arguing that finance theory (Black-Scholes, CAPM, efficient markets) did not merely *describe* markets - it *performed* them, becoming embedded in the plumbing, language, and legitimacy of modern finance.
- **Who should read it:** Markets/TradFi/crypto people who want the deep intellectual case for "models reshape the thing they model," and who can tolerate a scholarly (not pop-business) treatment. Not a quick airport read.
- **Time investment:** High. ~370–410 pages of dense but well-written academic prose; budget real reading time. Skimmable by chapter if you only want the performativity argument and the 1987/LTCM cases.

---

## Core concept: performativity ("an engine, not a camera")

MacKenzie's central claim is that economic models are, in Milton Friedman's phrase that gives the book its title, **"an engine of inquiry, not a camera to reproduce empirical facts."** Models don't passively photograph a pre-existing market; they are an active engine that helps *bring markets into being* and changes how they behave. ([MIT Press blurb via search](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262633673/an-engine-not-a-camera/); [Google Books](https://books.google.com/books/about/An_Engine_Not_a_Camera.html?id=M3x5tvAwzrQC))

He argues finance theory became incorporated into market infrastructure in **three ways** ([EJPE review](https://ejpe.org/journal/article/view/11/8); [Kieran Healy, "The Scottish Verdict"](https://kieranhealy.org/publications/rev-mackenzie/)):
1. **Technically** - models embedded in trading/pricing software, risk management, market organization, and regulation.
2. **Linguistically** - originally theoretical terms ("beta," "volatility," "implied volatility") became the everyday jargon of practitioners.
3. **Legitimizing** - financial economists helped make options and derivatives trading respectable at a time when it was viewed as little better than gambling.

### Degrees of performativity (the theoretical machinery)
MacKenzie distinguishes several grades of performativity. The strongest and most contested is **"Barnesian" performativity** (named for sociologist Barry Barnes, of the Edinburgh "strong programme" in sociology of scientific knowledge): the use of a model actively alters economic processes *in the direction of greater conformity to the model* - i.e., the theory helps make itself true. He also names the inverse, **"counterperformativity"**: use of a model that undermines its own assumptions and helps make itself *less* true. ([Healy review](https://kieranhealy.org/publications/rev-mackenzie/); [orgtheory discussion](https://orgtheory.wordpress.com/2007/01/08/mackenzie-redux/))

MacKenzie is careful to bound the claim: performativity does *not* mean any arbitrary formula can will itself true. Formulas that lead users to lose money or that open arbitrage opportunities tend to stop holding sway - there is a reality check. ([Search synthesis of reviews](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/237519544_Review_of_Donald_MacKenzie's_An_engine_not_a_camera_how_financial_models_shape_the_markets))

## Key cases

- **Black-Scholes-Merton option pricing** - the book's strongest evidence. MacKenzie (building on his 2003 paper with Yuval Millo) traces how the formula began as a *description* of option prices and, as it was inscribed into traders' sheets, routines, and software, increasingly *enacted* the very price patterns it predicted - until the 1987 crash. ([Search synthesis](https://www.amazon.com/Engine-Not-Camera-Financial-Technology/dp/0262633671); [EJPE review](https://ejpe.org/journal/article/view/11/8))
- **The 1987 crash ("Black Monday")** and the **1998 collapse of Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM)** - MacKenzie reads both as cases of **counterperformativity**, where widespread reliance on similar models and strategies (e.g., portfolio insurance; convergence arbitrage) contributed to the very dislocations the models assumed away. On LTCM he rejects several popular explanations; reviewers single out his "superportfolio" account of imitative crowding as among his more debatable suggestions. ([magic-maths-money review](http://magic-maths-money.blogspot.com/2011/08/engine-not-camera-how-financial-models.html); [search synthesis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/237519544_Review_of_Donald_MacKenzie's_An_engine_not_a_camera_how_financial_models_shape_the_markets))
- **History of financial derivatives and finance theory** - the rise of the Chicago options exchanges, the Capital Asset Pricing Model, efficient-market theory, and the Nobel-winning theorists (Black, Scholes, Merton, and others), told as intertwined intellectual and institutional history. ([Google Books](https://books.google.com/books/about/An_Engine_Not_a_Camera.html?id=M3x5tvAwzrQC))

## Structure and argument

The book is a roughly chronological history-of-ideas plus sociological analysis: it builds the empirical history of postwar financial economics (CAPM, efficient markets, Black-Scholes-Merton, derivatives exchanges), then uses it to develop and test the performativity thesis, culminating in the 1987 crash and LTCM as stress tests (counterperformativity). It draws heavily on archival work and on MacKenzie's own oral-history interviews with the economists and practitioners involved. Specific chapter titles/order could not be fully verified from the sources consulted (the full table of contents was not directly retrieved). ([EJPE review](https://ejpe.org/journal/article/view/11/8); [uberty full-text PDF](https://uberty.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/MacKenzie-An-Engine-Not-a-Camera.pdf))

## Author background

**Donald Angus MacKenzie** (b. 3 May 1950) is Professor of Sociology at the **University of Edinburgh**, where he has taught since 1975 (Professor since 1992; BSc 1972 and PhD 1978, both Edinburgh). He is a leading figure in the **sociology of science and technology (STS)**. Earlier books include *Inventing Accuracy* (1990, on nuclear-missile guidance), *Knowing Machines* (1996), and *Mechanizing Proof* (2001), all MIT Press. Honors: Fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh (2001), Fellow of the British Academy (2004), and the Society for Social Studies of Science's **Bernal Prize** (2005). ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_MacKenzie_(sociologist)); [Edinburgh SPS staff page](https://www.sps.ed.ac.uk/staff/donald-mackenzie))

## Difficulty level (honest take)

This is a **scholarly book**, not popular finance writing. It originates in science-and-technology studies and economic sociology, and engages explicitly with the "strong programme" in the sociology of scientific knowledge. Reviewers consistently call it "incisive, well-written, and accessible" *for what it is*, and praise MacKenzie's genuine command of the technical finance - but also warn that a reader unfamiliar with the intellectual history of the "strong programme" may pass over the core concept of **Barnesian performativity** without grasping it. Expect some equations and a lot of intellectual history. Accessible to a motivated general reader; demanding for a casual one. ([Healy review](https://kieranhealy.org/publications/rev-mackenzie/); [orgtheory](https://orgtheory.wordpress.com/2007/01/08/mackenzie-redux/))

## Reception in economic sociology

Widely regarded as a landmark and a foundational text of the **"performativity" turn** in economic sociology and the social studies of finance; it is among MacKenzie's most-cited works and helped define the field. Portions of the work won the **Viviana A. Zelizer Prize** in economic sociology from the American Sociological Association. It was reviewed in major outlets including the *American Journal of Sociology*, the *European Journal for the Philosophy of Economics*, and *Socio-Economic Review*. ([search synthesis](https://www.amazon.com/Engine-Not-Camera-Financial-Technology/dp/0262633671); [AJS](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/518053); [Socio-Economic Review](https://academic.oup.com/ser/article/8/2/399/1631320)) On Goodreads it holds **4.30/5 across 171 ratings** (15 written reviews) - a small but very positive lay sample. ([Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/134474.An_Engine_Not_a_Camera))

**Critiques.** The most common pushback is that the *strong* (Barnesian) version of performativity - that economics "creates the phenomena it describes" - is asserted more than fully proven, and that some readers will miss the concept entirely. Kieran Healy's review essay "The Scottish Verdict" is notably *ambivalent*: he judges the evidence for performativity to be about as good as the case for orthodox financial economics, without claiming it is overwhelming. The LTCM "superportfolio" mechanism is flagged as one of the weaker arguments. ([Healy](https://kieranhealy.org/publications/rev-mackenzie/); [orgtheory](https://orgtheory.wordpress.com/2007/01/08/mackenzie-redux/); [search synthesis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/237519544_Review_of_Donald_MacKenzie's_An_engine_not_a_camera_how_financial_models_shape_the_markets))

## Why a markets / crypto / TradFi reader might value it

- It is the canonical, evidence-based statement of a phenomenon crypto and quant readers feel intuitively: **when a model is adopted widely enough, it reshapes the market and can become self-fulfilling - or self-defeating.** That is directly relevant to algorithmic/automated trading, oracle-fed pricing, stablecoin pegs, AMM curves, and "reflexive" feedback in DeFi.
- **Counterperformativity** (1987, LTCM) is a clean historical template for model-driven crowding and cascade risk - useful for thinking about correlated liquidations, basis-trade unwinds, and homogeneous strategies.
- It supplies the intellectual lineage behind the a16z framing ("what happens when theories stop describing the world and start changing it"), and shows the same dynamic playing out across a prior wave of financial innovation (derivatives) - a useful analogy for crypto-as-new-asset-class and its legitimation struggle.
- Caveat: it is history-and-theory, not a how-to; readers wanting trading tactics or crypto-specific analysis will not find them here.
